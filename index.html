Identify the Group

Who They Were

Chicanos were Mexican Americans — many of them farmworkers, students, and urban youth — who, in the 1960s, reclaimed a word once used as an insult and turned it into a banner of pride and political identity.

They lived between two worlds — rooted in Mexican heritage yet born of the United States — and frequently faced segregation, low wages, and exclusion from the political process. The movement gave that shared experience a name, a flag, and a voice.

Goals of the Movement

The Cause

What were Chicanos asking for? The movement was never a single demand but a braided set of struggles — over the fields, the classroom, the ballot box, and the right to belong.

✊

Labor & Wages

Fair pay, safe conditions, and the right to unionize for farmworkers exploited in the fields of the Southwest.

✺

Land & Justice

Recovery of land grants promised to Mexican families and stripped away after 1848.

✎

Education

An end to segregated, underfunded schools — and a curriculum that taught Mexican American history and culture.

☼

Identity & Pride

Reclaiming Chicano as a source of dignity and forging a shared cultural homeland: Aztlán.

▲

Political Power

Representation through new vehicles like La Raza Unida — a party run by and for the community.

Key Events

Flashpoints

The moments that turned a community's frustration into a national movement.

  1. 1965

    The Delano Grape Strike

    Filipino and Mexican farmworkers walk off the vineyards of California's Central Valley, launching a five-year strike and national grape boycott.

  2. 1968

    The East L.A. Walkouts

    Tens of thousands of students walk out of Los Angeles high schools demanding equal education — the “Blowouts.”

  3. 1969

    National Chicano Youth Conference

    In Denver, young activists adopt El Plan Espiritual de Aztlán, declaring cultural unity and self-determination.

  4. 1970

    The Chicano Moratorium

    Over 20,000 march in Los Angeles against the Vietnam War and its toll on the Chicano community; journalist Rubén Salazar is killed.

Legacy

The Eagle Still Flies

By 1973 the movement had reshaped American life — farm labor law, bilingual education, Chicano studies, and a generation of leaders who refused to be invisible. ¡La lucha continúa!
